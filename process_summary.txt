  - fully nonparametric AIPW DML for the eligibility ATE,
  - PLIV DML for the participation LATE,
  - fully nonparametric LATE DML for the binary-instrument / binary-treatment case.
- Kept the new helper design consistent with the existing notebook style: pandas inputs, fold-based cross-fitting, and score-based inference.

Thinking Overview

1. Model choice logic
- The assignment allowed sklearn-based nuisance learners and emphasized offline stability.
- I tested both lasso-based and random-forest-based nuisance configurations outside the notebook before finalizing the inserted notebook code.
- The lasso path was workable but much slower because of repeated inner cross-validation in multiple nuisance stages.
- A modest random-forest default produced estimates in the expected empirical range while remaining stable and simple enough for notebook execution.
- I therefore used random forests for the new fully nonparametric estimators and kept the notebook's existing sparse methods for the benchmark comparisons.

2. Why these estimators were added
- Question 1 required a cross-fitted AIPW estimator with nuisance functions for treated outcome regression, untreated outcome regression, and treatment propensity. That estimator directly targets the fully nonparametric ATE.
- Question 2 required the partially linear IV orthogonal score. That is different from the PLR estimator already in the notebook, so it needed a new implementation.
- Question 3 required the nonparametric Wald-type orthogonal score using outcome bridge terms and treatment bridge terms by instrument status. That also required a separate implementation.

3. Validation approach
- Before patching the notebook, I ran standalone checks on the 401(k) data to make sure the estimates were sensible.
- I compared them against the assignment sanity-check ranges:
  - naive eligibility effect around $19.6k,
  - flexible ATE estimates around $7k-$9k,
  - IV/LATE participation effects around $9k-$12k.
- The candidate implementations produced results in those ranges, so I used them in the notebook.
